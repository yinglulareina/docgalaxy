# Consciousness

Consciousness is the subjective, first-person experience of being – the fact that there is "something it is like" to be you. It is one of the deepest puzzles in philosophy and science.

## The Hard Problem

David Chalmers distinguishes the "easy problems" of consciousness (explaining attention, memory, reportability) from the "hard problem": why physical processes give rise to subjective experience at all. Even a complete neuroscientific account of how the brain processes information would leave unexplained why that processing feels like anything from the inside.

## Major Theories

**Global Workspace Theory** (Baars, Dehaene) proposes that consciousness corresponds to information being broadcast widely across the brain. Content is "conscious" when it enters a global workspace accessible to many cognitive systems simultaneously.

**Integrated Information Theory** (Tononi) defines consciousness as integrated information (Φ). Any system with high Φ – meaning the system as a whole generates more information than the sum of its parts – is conscious to a degree proportional to its Φ value.

**Higher-Order Theories** argue that a mental state is conscious when it is the object of a higher-order representation. You are conscious of seeing red because you have a thought about your perception of red.

## The Binding Problem

How does the brain unify disparate perceptual features (the color, shape, and motion of an object) into a single coherent experience? Neural signals are processed in distinct regions, yet perception is unified.

Solving the hard problem may require concepts not yet in our scientific vocabulary – or may reveal that our intuitions about consciousness are fundamentally confused.

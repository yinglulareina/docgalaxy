# The Mind-Body Problem

How does the mind relate to the body? This ancient question, sharpened by Descartes' dualism, remains central to philosophy of mind.

## Substance Dualism

Descartes proposed that mind and body are distinct substances: the body is extended (takes up space) while the mind is unextended (purely thinking). This immediately raises the interaction problem: if they're fundamentally different, how do they causally influence each other? How does a decision (mental) cause a hand to move (physical)?

## Physicalism

The dominant view in contemporary philosophy. Mental states are either identical to physical states (type physicalism) or realized by physical states without being identical to them (functionalism). On this view, pain just *is* a certain pattern of neural firing – nothing over and above.

## Functionalism

A mental state is defined by its functional role: its causal relations to inputs, outputs, and other mental states. Pain is whatever state is caused by tissue damage, causes avoidance behavior, and interacts with beliefs and desires in characteristic ways. Multiple different physical systems could implement the same functional state.

## Property Dualism

There is only one substance (physical), but it has two irreducibly different kinds of properties: physical properties and phenomenal (mental) properties. Pain is a physical process, but its painfulness – the way it feels – is a distinct phenomenal property not captured by physical description alone.

## Eliminativism

Folk psychological concepts like "belief" and "desire" are theoretical posits of a folk theory that will be replaced by a mature neuroscience. There are no beliefs; there are only neural states.

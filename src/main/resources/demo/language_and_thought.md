# Language and Thought

Does language shape thought, or does thought exist independently of language? The relationship between linguistic and cognitive structure is one of the most debated questions in philosophy of mind and cognitive science.

## The Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis

Strong version (linguistic determinism): language determines thought. We can only think thoughts our language gives us words for. This extreme form is widely rejected – pre-linguistic infants and non-human animals clearly have thoughts.

Weak version (linguistic relativity): language influences thought. The language you speak shapes certain aspects of how you perceive and categorize the world. Evidence includes cross-linguistic differences in color discrimination, spatial reasoning, and time perception. English speakers think of time as horizontal; Mandarin speakers as vertical.

## Language of Thought Hypothesis

Fodor proposed that thought is computation over mental representations with a language-like structure (Mentalese). Thinking in English or Chinese is surface froth; all thinkers share a common underlying representational system. This explains the productivity and systematicity of thought: we can think an unbounded number of thoughts because we have combinatorial rules, just like natural language grammar.

## Embodied Cognition

Critics of Fodor argue that concepts are grounded in sensorimotor experience, not arbitrary symbols. The concept of "grasping" an idea derives meaning from the bodily experience of grasping physical objects. Metaphor is not ornamental but constitutive of thought.

## Inner Speech

Many people report thinking "in words." Inner speech activates the same motor and auditory cortex regions as overt speech. But inner speech seems to presuppose thought – you have to know what you want to say before you say it, even internally.

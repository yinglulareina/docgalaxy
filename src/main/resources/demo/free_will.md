# Free Will

Free will is the capacity to choose among alternatives and bear moral responsibility for those choices. Whether humans genuinely possess it is one of philosophy's most contested questions.

## The Determinist Challenge

Classical determinism holds that every event, including every human decision, is the inevitable result of prior causes governed by physical laws. If the state of the universe at t determines its state at t+1, then your "choice" of what to eat for breakfast was fixed at the Big Bang. There is no room for the kind of freedom that grounds moral responsibility.

## Compatibilism

The most widely held view among contemporary philosophers. Compatibilists argue that free will and determinism are not in conflict. What matters for free will is not the absence of causation but the absence of *compulsion*. A person acts freely when they act from their own desires and reasoning, even if those desires are themselves determined. Harry Frankfurt's work on hierarchical desires is influential here.

## Hard Incompatibilism

Some argue that free will requires indeterminism – events that are genuinely undetermined. But even if quantum mechanics introduces fundamental randomness, random events don't seem to ground the kind of control we associate with freedom. Neither determinism nor indeterminism seems to leave room for authentic agency.

## Moral Responsibility

The stakes are high. If there is no free will, can we hold anyone morally responsible for their actions? Most compatibilists argue yes – what matters is whether the agent was responsive to reasons. Punishment and praise can still modify behavior even in a deterministic world.

Pereboom and others argue for "hard incompatibilism with revisionary moral practices" – accepting no robust free will while redesigning institutions around rehabilitation rather than retribution.

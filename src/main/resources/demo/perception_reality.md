# Perception and Reality

Perception is the process by which sensory information is organized and interpreted to yield our experience of the world. But how faithfully does perception represent reality?

## Naive Realism

The commonsense view: we perceive the world as it is. When you see a red apple, there really is a red apple with redness as one of its properties, and you are directly in contact with it.

## Representative Realism (Indirect Realism)

We only directly perceive mental representations (sense data, percepts) caused by external objects. The external world is inferred, not directly experienced. This faces the veil-of-perception problem: how can we know the external world if all we ever encounter are our own representations?

## Phenomenology: Perception as Active Construction

Merleau-Ponty argued that perception is not passive reception but active, embodied engagement with the world. The body is not an object we perceive but the very medium through which we perceive. Perception is shaped by our habits, skills, and purposes.

## Predictive Processing

Contemporary cognitive science proposes that perception is Bayesian inference: the brain constantly generates predictions about sensory input (top-down) and updates those predictions based on prediction errors (bottom-up). What we consciously experience is the brain's best hypothesis about the world, not a raw copy of it.

## Perceptual Relativity

The same object looks different under different lighting, from different angles, through different media. Color perception varies across species. This suggests perception is not a transparent window onto a mind-independent reality but a species-relative, context-sensitive construction.
